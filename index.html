Vaccine disparities continue in West Harlem in wake of new COVID-19 booster

By Sophie Dau

October 11, 2022

Photo by Shannon Binns

Every Wednesday this spring, families lined up on Malcolm X Boulevard to receive their vaccines from a van blasting music. Kids anxiously waited their turn—not out of fear for a shot, but out of excitement because they would get to choose a basketball, or maybe a soccer ball, as a prize. If the lines were long, chairs were brought out. If it was hot, there was water. On Martin Luther King Jr.’s birthday, organizers handed out free books, including books of Amanda Gorman’s poetry.

This is the vaccine experience created by Dave Crenshaw, or “Coach Dave,” a community organizer who runs Uptown Dreamers, an organization committed to providing resources to “left out” communities. He wanted the center, which partners with Black Health, to serve as a place for community information; besides vaccines, residents could come with any health questions and receive screenings for anything from blood pressure to mammograms.

“Everyone doesn’t always treat the people correct,” Crenshaw said. “But we tried to make sure we were treating them—we didn’t want to treat them like, ‘You are getting something free, you should take it whenever we give it to you.’ No, we treated them with respect. We tried to make sure they have an enjoyable experience, and they were treated with dignity and respect. That was the key.”

By building trust as a recognizable community leader and working to make information on the COVID-19 vaccine readily available, Crenshaw and his team were able to provide safe and accessible health centers. Despite such extensive community outreach, though, data from almost two and a half years into the pandemic reflects continuing vaccine disparities between West Harlem and other neighborhoods in Manhattan.

In the 10027 zip code, which includes parts of Morningside Heights, Central Harlem, and West Harlem, overall vaccination rates are lower than in the Upper West Side and Midtown neighborhoods. 73 percent of 10027 residents have completed the first vaccine series; in comparison, residents of the 10036 zip code in Midtown, a generally whiter and more affluent neighborhood, are vaccinated at a rate of 99 percent.

Medical experts see a number of variables contributing to this vaccine disparity. Robert Fullilove, a professor at Columbia Mailman School of Public Health and a faculty member of the Columbia University Irving Medical Center, said community distrust is a major factor in the lower vaccine rate.

“When we’re trying to bring public health programs and public health prevention measures to the community, there’s always a lot of resistance, and there’s a lot of doubt about whether or not, once again, we’re being sincere in saying that we have the health of the community at heart,” he said.

Fullilove noted that the history of anti-Blackness in healthcare has led to a tenuous relationship between Black communities and hospitals. West and Central Harlem, which are predominantly Black neighborhoods, have experienced this mistreatment firsthand. Since this pandemic involved the government placing many restrictions on people’s lives, such as masking and testing, Fullilove said the community may be skeptical about whether they are a priority.

Yet, James Colgrove, a professor of public health at the Mailman School of Public Health, points to data showing that Black families are not necessarily disproportionately averse to vaccines compared to other demographic groups in the country. He provides another explanation for this disparity: lack of access.

“If [people] see low immunization rates in a community, they’re quick to say, ‘Oh, that’s because these people are hesitant’ when in fact, they may not be hesitant at all. They may be very eager to be vaccinated, but for whatever logistical or economic reasons, they just can’t,” he said. “They work two jobs and they can’t get off work to take their kids to a clinic. They don’t have a clinic that's convenient to them. They haven’t signed up for Medicaid, their insurance. For whatever reason, they may not be able to actually get to the vaccination.”

When the first COVID-19 vaccination plans were originally announced in December 2020, then-mayor Bill de Blasio promised to prioritize neighborhoods with a history of experiencing racial inequality. Although West Harlem was included as one such neighborhood, by February 2021, residents still did not have a single vaccination site. Over a decade earlier, during the swine flu pandemic of 2009, Columbia received vaccine doses over a month before local West Harlem pharmacies. To combat this history of inequity, medical professionals and community advocates alike emphasize the importance of convenient vaccination locations and accessible information, especially for a multi-shot series of vaccines.

Beyond a comparative gap in COVID-19 vaccine data, the rate of vaccination also decreases for each additional shot between Morningside Heights and Central and West Harlem. Starting at 86.62 percent for one dose and 73 percent for two doses, the additional booster rate drops to 38.86 percent for 10027 residents. Both Fullilove and Colgrove see this decrease as a natural result of long-term diseases—for this pandemic, the phenomenon is often dubbed “COVID fatigue.”

“It’s becoming very difficult to separate attitudes towards getting a vaccine for the regular old flu to make people see this particular virus, COVID-19, is different, and you definitely [have] to get a booster,” Fullilove said. “I think it’s really an uphill battle at this point.”

Fullilove sees Harlem residents starting to treat the boosters like a flu vaccine: something people skip not because of anti-vaccination sentiments, but because it is not a priority. This phenomenon is not unique to the neighborhood; across the city, booster vaccination rates are declining.

“I think what these outbreaks really demonstrate is that successful vaccination programs almost inevitably become a victim of their success,” Colgrove said. “That is just a feature of vaccines: the better they work, the more people [think they] don’t need them anymore. So it’s very hard to maintain high rates of vaccination coverage over time. It requires constant efforts on the part of healthcare professionals, on the part of the public health system to maintain that level of uptake.”

This warning is especially poignant when examining another key element of the data: the case and death rate. The 10027 zip code area has a lower number of cases but higher rates of death due to COVID relative to Midtown, with 29,879.04 cases per 100,000 people and 373.85 deaths per 100,000 people as of Sunday. West Midtown neighborhoods have a case rate of 54,206.43 per 100,000 people and death rate of 319.36 per 100,000 people.

Both Fullilove and Colgrove point to underlying health conditions to explain these uneven disparities. The data on health conditions follows this logic too—Morningside Heights, Hamilton Heights, and Central Harlem all have higher rates of obesity, high blood pressure, and diabetes than the rest of Manhattan. Similarly, all three neighborhoods have much higher “avoidable” hospitalizations, which are defined by the New York Sstate Department of Health as hospitalizations that could have been prevented with access to quality primary care.

“So like most folks in public health, I always point to the health disparities that were already present in many communities in the United States that were aggravated by COVID-19 infections, because it’s the history of health in the community plus the arrival of COVID-19 that accounts for high rates of mortality, even in areas where you didn’t have as many cases as we’re seeing elsewhere,” Fullilove said.

To combat the disparities caused by preexisting health inequities facing Harlem residents, many local community organizations are working to spread information about the vaccine and build trust.

One such organization, Public Health Solutions, has recently taken over the Harlem Health Advocacy Partners program, which aims to improve the health and well-being of New York City Housing Authority residents in Harlem. One of its priorities has been providing free and convenient vaccine sites to residents. Arielle Burlett, the director of healthcare-community partnerships, explained in a statement that many residents rely primarily on vaccine information that spreads through “word of mouth” from community members they trust to help inform their decisions. Although new to West Harlem sites, the program’s outreach has made it a valuable resource for the community.

“From the [community health workers’] experience promoting the previous booster, there were many people who returned to the East Harlem Neighborhood Health Action Center because they trusted the site and staff after receiving the first two doses,” she wrote. “It was also free and convenient.”

Public Health Solutions also acts as a resource for community concerns, answering questions ranging from “​​Who’s providing the vaccine?” to “Should I get vaccinated if I’m pregnant?" and “​​What’s in the vaccine?”

Fullilove too values the importance of highlighting voices within the community. He is a senior adviser to the Bard Prison Initiative on public health programs, where he has taught public health courses and helped six graduates from his program earn graduate degrees from the Mailman School of Public Health.

“My objective has always been to have members of the formerly incarcerated community in New York City, who are interested in doing community work, to become involved in the many, many programs that are built and created to advance the cause of public health and the community,” he said.

In light of issues of health inequity, leaders like Crenshaw, from Uptown Dreamers, are especially vital to fostering a healthy and safe neighborhood. Crenshaw has worked hard to become a trusted member of Harlem; his connections have helped his organization combat the vaccine barriers his community faces.

“No one got upset with me as if they had a bad reaction, that they can’t do this, or they can’t do that, anything else like that,” Crenshaw said. “So I tell people I have direct data. … And my community is very happy the vans have been here.”
